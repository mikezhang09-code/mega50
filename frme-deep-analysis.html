Understanding the Business
理解生意 — 印第安纳州地区社区银行

"Our return on assets, return on tangible capital, and efficiency ratio are in the top-quartile relative to our peers, reflecting disciplined execution and operational excellence."

— Mark Hardwick, CEO, Q3 2025 Earnings

First Merchants Corporation is a community-focused regional bank headquartered in Muncie, Indiana, operating through First Merchants Bank with 127 banking centers across Indiana, Ohio, and Michigan. Founded in 1893, it is one of the oldest continuously operating banks in the Midwest and ranks #1 in market deposit share in Muncie MSA, #3 in Fort Wayne, #4 in Lake County (Northwest Indiana), and #1 in Monroe County, Michigan. The bank's strategy is relationship banking — building multi-decade ties with local businesses and high-net-worth families.

How the Business Makes Money 商业模式

FRME earns money the way community banks have for centuries: borrow short (deposits), lend long (loans), capture the spread, and add fee income from wealth management and treasury services. The key differentiator is relationship banking — courting local business owners and HNW families with personalized service, rather than competing on price with mega-banks. This produces stickier deposits (lower funding cost over time) and better credit selection (knowing the borrower personally reduces losses). Specialty verticals added through M&A — like the First Savings triple-net-lease and first-lien HELOC books — provide higher-margin growth pockets.

03
Intrinsic Value
内在价值

For a bank, three valuation methods are most appropriate: (1) Justified P/TBV based on ROTCE; (2) Dividend Discount Model (DDM); (3) Conservative DCF on owner earnings. Use multiple methods and triangulate.

Method 1: Justified P/TBV Multiple 合理市净率法

Justified P/TBV = (ROTCE − g) / (Cost of Equity − g)

Inputs:
  ROTCE = 14.08% (FY25 actual; normalized 13-15%)
  g = 5% (mid-cycle TBVPS growth)
  Cost of Equity = 10% (10yr UST 4.3% + 5.7% equity risk premium)

Justified P/TBV = (0.1408 − 0.05) / (0.10 − 0.05) = 1.82×

TBVPS (Q1 2026) = $29.34
Implied fair value = 1.82 × $29.34 = $53.40 per share

Method 2: Dividend Discount Model (DDM) 股息贴现模型

Multi-stage DDM:
  Stage 1 (Y1-5): Dividend grows 5% (recent track record)
  Stage 2 (Y6+): Steady-state growth 3% (GDP-aligned)
  Required return: 9.5% (bank discount rate)

Year 1 dividend = $1.44 × 1.05 = $1.51
PV of stage-1 dividends (Y1-5) = ~$6.50
Terminal value at Year 5 = D6 / (0.095 − 0.03) = $1.94 / 0.065 = $29.85
PV of terminal = $29.85 / 1.095^5 = $19.00

DDM Intrinsic Value = $6.50 + $19.00 = $25.50 (DDM understates because FRME retains 63% of earnings for compounding)

Note: Pure DDM understates value for banks that retain meaningful capital. Use as floor reference.

Competitive Moat
护城河分析

Banks have narrow but durable moats. The economics of regional banking favor incumbents because of switching costs, regulatory licensing, and scale-vs-area. FRME's moat sources, ranked by strength:

Customer Switching CostsCustomers rarely move primary checking, payroll, treasury management, or business loan relationships. Inertia is the most durable bank moat. FRME's 132-year operating history in Indiana means many multi-generational relationships.STRONG
Local Market Density (Efficient Scale)FRME holds #1 deposit share in Muncie, #1 in Monroe County MI, #2 in Lafayette, #3 in Fort Wayne, #4 in Lake County. In specific micro-markets, FRME has scale advantages even if it lacks national scale. New entrants would destroy returns.STRONG

Technology / Network EffectsMobile banking competitive but not differentiating. FRME spends adequately on tech but is not a tech innovator. Possible long-term threat from fintech and digital-only banks.WEAK

Moat Trend: Stable to Slightly Widening 护城河趋势：稳定至微扩

Widening signals: Efficiency ratio improved from 63% (2023) to 54.5% (2025). NIM expanding (3.25% FY25 → 3.30% Q1 26). Loan growth 7.3% in 2025. M&A footprint expansion (Indianapolis, Columbus, Detroit, now Louisville/Southern Indiana).

Stable signals: Top-quartile ROA/ROTCE/efficiency for 5+ years. NCO ratio 0.10% — disciplined credit selection. CET1 capital well above regulatory minimums.

Threats: (1) Fintech / digital banks chipping at consumer deposits; (2) Mega-bank scale advantages on tech and product breadth; (3) Office CRE secular weakness ($12M maturing exposure disclosed).

Munger Inversion: What Could Destroy This Moat? 芒格反向思维

1. Mass digital banking adoption wipes out branch-based switching costs (10+ year horizon, gradual).
2. Major credit cycle in Indiana — concentrated in one state's economy, an industrial-heavy state.
3. Regulatory burden increase disproportionately hurts mid-size banks vs both small (exempt) and large (can absorb).
4. Botched acquisition integration — FRME has done many M&A; First Savings is the latest.

None of these are imminent. The moat is durable for at least 10 years.

Moat Verdict: Narrow but Durable 窄护城河 — 持久

FRME has a narrow but durable moat rooted in switching costs, local-market density, and regulatory protection. It is not a wide-moat business like Coca-Cola or Visa, but it earns 14% ROTCE consistently — well above its ~10% cost of equity — which is exactly the definition of a moat in financial terms. The moat is wider in core markets (Muncie, Monroe County) and narrower in larger metros (Indianapolis, Detroit) where mega-banks compete. Moat Score: 21/30 (Narrow Moat).

Risk Assessment
风险评估

1. Geographic Concentration 地域集中风险

~70% of business in Indiana, with material exposure to Ohio and Michigan. Indiana's economy is industrial-manufacturing heavy (auto suppliers, RV makers, agri-business). A regional recession would hit FRME's loan book disproportionately. Mitigant: Diversified borrowers within state; conservative underwriting (NCO 0.10%); current allowance for credit losses 1.6% of loans.

2. Office CRE Exposure 办公地产风险

Two office credits totaling ~$12M flagged as nonperforming. Office CRE remains structurally weak. Q4 2024 management flagged "loans maturing in less than a year represent 25% of office portfolio." Mitigant: Total office exposure is small relative to $15.3B loan book. Has been transparent and proactive in disclosure.

3. Acquisition Integration 并购整合风险

First Savings closed Feb 2026 — Q1 2026 had $17M acquisition-related charges + $29.8M mark-to-market on mortgage repositioning. Track record on prior M&A is solid but each deal carries integration risk. Mitigant: Tangible book earnback of 4 years on the mortgage repositioning trade is reasonable.

4. Interest Rate Sensitivity 利率敏感度

NIM is sensitive to Fed policy. FY25 NIM expanded during rate cuts because deposit costs repriced faster than asset yields. A reversal — sharply rising rates — would temporarily compress NIM. Mitigant: Diversified asset book; bond portfolio cash flows ($276M in 2026) being redeployed into higher-yielding loans.

5. Banking Sector Tail Risk 银行业尾部风险

2023 SVB-era failures showed that even well-capitalized banks can face deposit runs in panic episodes. Mitigant: FRME's deposit base is granular (no >5% single depositor), CET1 well above regulatory minimums, $3.3B bond portfolio provides liquidity buffer.

6. Digital / Fintech Disruption 数字银行颠覆

Long-term threat to community banks: digital-only banks erode deposit franchise; fintech lenders compete on consumer credit. Mitigant: 10+ year horizon; relationship banking with HNW and SMB customers harder to disrupt than retail consumer; FRME spending adequately on tech.

Value Trap Diagnostic 价值陷阱诊断

Is FRME a value trap? Apply the diagnostic:

❌ Earnings power declining? No — record FY25, expanding NIM, growing loans.
❌ Industry in secular decline? No — community banking is mature but stable; the "post-2023 banking discount" is sentiment, not structural.
❌ Management destroying capital? No — buybacks below TBV, sensible M&A, growing book value.
❌ Balance sheet weak? No — CET1 ~11%, NCO 0.10%, granular deposits.
❌ Earnings quality issues? No — Q1 2026 charges were transparently disclosed; adjusted EPS $1.03 vs reported $0.45 fully explained.

Conclusion: NOT a value trap. This is a compounder priced as if it were one.

FRME is moderately neglected — not deeply hidden, but well below large-cap institutional radar. The neglect drivers are:
