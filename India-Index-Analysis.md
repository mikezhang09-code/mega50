# India's Sensex delivered S&P 500–level returns while most global markets lagged

**India's BSE Sensex compounded at roughly 12% annually in rupee terms from 1997 to 2025, and approximately 9.5–10% in US dollar terms — matching the S&P 500's total return over the same period.** This is remarkable because virtually every other major developed and emerging market index delivered substantially lower dollar-denominated returns over these 28 years. The FTSE 100 managed roughly 5% annualized, the Nikkei 225 about 4.5%, Hong Kong's Hang Seng around 5%, and Shanghai's Composite just 4.3% in local currency (roughly 2.5–3% in dollar terms). India's outperformance rests on a rare convergence of structural tailwinds — a 10x expansion in nominal GDP, the world's youngest major-economy workforce, an IT exports boom that grew revenues 50-fold, and a domestic investor revolution that turned 207 million demat accounts into a permanent liquidity engine. No other large emerging market assembled all these ingredients simultaneously.

---

## How the numbers stack up across 28 years

The Sensex rose from **3,659 in 1997 to approximately 85,000 by late 2025**, a roughly 23-fold increase producing an **11.9% price CAGR in rupees**. Adding dividends pushes total returns to about 13.2% annualized. But the rupee depreciated from ₹36 to roughly ₹87 per dollar over this period — a 3.1% annual drag — which pulls the dollar-denominated total return to approximately **9.7–10%**, essentially matching the S&P 500's long-run 10% total return.

Most other major markets delivered far less in dollar terms:

| Index | Local CAGR (price) | Approx. USD total return CAGR | Key issue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **BSE Sensex** | 11.9% | ~9.5–10% | Rupee depreciation offset |
| **S&P 500** | 7.2% | ~10% | Benchmark |
| **FTSE 100** | 1.6% | ~5% | Sterling weakness, low growth |
| **Nikkei 225** | 4.3% | ~4.5% | Lost decades, yen collapse |
| **Hang Seng** | 3.2% | ~5–6% | HKD peg; China drag post-2021 |
| **Shanghai Composite** | 4.3% | ~3–4% | Still 35% below 2007 peak |
| **Ibovespa** | 9.5% | ~3–5% | Real collapsed repeatedly |
| **DAX** | ~6.4% (TR) | ~5.5–6% | Euro depreciation |

The MSCI India Index confirms the pattern: it returned **10.79% annualized in USD since December 2000**, outperforming both the MSCI Emerging Markets (8.52%) and the MSCI ACWI. Between 2014 and 2024, India outperformed the broader EM index in 8 of 11 calendar years. India's weight in the MSCI EM Index surged from 8% to roughly 20% in six years, overtaking Taiwan, while China's weight shrank from above 40% to around 25%.

The Sensex's journey through milestones tells its own story of accelerating momentum. It took **19 years** to climb from 10,000 (February 2006) to 40,000 (May 2019), but just **5 years** to double again to 80,000 (July 2024). The index set its all-time intraday high of **85,978 on September 27, 2024**, before correcting roughly 13% into early 2026.

## A ten-fold GDP expansion powered by demographics, IT, and reform

India's nominal GDP expanded from roughly **$420 billion in 1997 to $4.13 trillion by 2025** — a nearly ten-fold increase representing an 8.5–9% dollar CAGR. India leapfrogged from the world's 13th-largest economy to the **4th-largest**, surpassing Italy, Brazil, France, the UK, and most recently Japan. Real GDP growth averaged 6–6.5% in the early 2000s, accelerated to 7–7.5% mid-decade, and has sustained 6.5–7.5% in recent years, making India consistently the **fastest-growing major economy**.

**Demographics provided the foundational tailwind.** India's median age of 29 years compares with 38 in China, 38 in the US, and 48 in Japan. Over 65% of its 1.46 billion citizens are under 35, and its working-age population is projected to reach **1.04 billion by 2030**. The middle class expanded dramatically: households earning above $17/day grew from roughly 14% of the population in 2005 to **31% (432 million people) by 2021**, with projections reaching 715 million by 2031. This consumption engine drives nearly 70% of GDP — a critical structural advantage over export-dependent peers.

**The IT services boom generated transformative corporate earnings.** Indian IT exports mushroomed from under **$4 billion in 1998 to $224 billion by FY2025**, growing at roughly 15% annually for over two decades. The sector's contribution to GDP rose from 0.4% in 1991 to 7.7% by 2017, directly employing 5.4 million workers and capturing over 50% of the global IT outsourcing market. Companies like TCS and Infosys became globally competitive profit machines, generating high-margin dollar revenues that flowed directly into market capitalization growth.

**Continuous economic reform maintained momentum.** The 1991 liberalization dismantled the License Raj and opened India to foreign investment. Subsequent waves included GST unification in 2017 (creating a single national market), Make in India (2014), Digital India, Startup India (producing 126 unicorns by 2025), and Production-Linked Incentive schemes worth ₹1.6 lakh crore for semiconductors and electronics. Cumulative FDI inflows reached **$1.12 trillion between 2000 and 2025**. India's digital public infrastructure — UPI, Aadhaar (1.43 billion biometric IDs), and Jan Dhan (530 million bank accounts) — created a frictionless financial architecture unmatched globally. UPI processed **228 billion transactions worth $3.4 trillion in 2025**, handling roughly 50% of the world's real-time digital payment volume.

## The domestic investor revolution changed India's market structure

Perhaps the most consequential shift has been the **financialization of Indian household savings**. Mutual fund AUM exploded from roughly ₹0.5 trillion in 1997 to **₹82 trillion ($1 trillion) by early 2026** — a 160-fold increase. Systematic Investment Plans (SIPs) emerged as the defining mechanism: monthly SIP flows climbed from ₹8,000 crore in 2020 to a record **₹31,000 crore ($3.6 billion) by December 2025**, creating a consistent monthly equity demand floor. Active SIP accounts reached approximately 100 million.

Demat accounts surged from **23 million in 2015 to 207 million by September 2025** — a nine-fold increase driven by smartphone-based brokerages like Zerodha and Groww. The structural result was historic: **DII ownership of listed companies hit 17.82%, surpassing FII ownership of 17.23% for the first time in March 2025**. This shift means India's market is no longer hostage to foreign capital flows. When FIIs executed record net selling of ₹1.52 lakh crore ($18 billion) in 2025, domestic investors absorbed the selling pressure and limited the Sensex correction to roughly 13% from peak.

The banking sector's transformation reinforced this story. Gross NPAs collapsed from a crisis peak of **11.2% in 2018 to just 2.15% by September 2025** — one of the most dramatic banking turnarounds globally. SEBI's regulatory modernization — including T+1 settlement (making India the first major market to achieve this), derivatives market development, and enhanced corporate governance norms — built institutional trust that attracted both domestic and international capital.

**Reliance Industries** transformed from a petrochemicals conglomerate into a diversified digital-energy-retail giant. The 2016 launch of Jio — which attracted 506 million mobile subscribers — was a pivotal moment, essentially building India's digital infrastructure. **Bharti Airtel** emerged as the biggest wealth creator from 2020 to 2025, adding ₹7.9 lakh crore in market value as India's telecom sector consolidated. **Bajaj Finance** pioneered consumer lending at scale, growing AUM at a 30% CAGR from ₹24,000 crore to ₹3.3 lakh crore while revenue compounded at nearly 30% annually.

For US-based investors, several Indian companies trade as ADRs: **Infosys (INFY)** on NYSE (the first Indian company to list on NASDAQ in 1999), **HDFC Bank (HDB)**, **ICICI Bank (IBN)** — the first Indian bank on NYSE in 1999 — **Wipro (WIT)**, **Dr. Reddy's (RDY)**, and **MakeMyTrip (MMYT)** on NASDAQ. INFY delivered roughly 8% annualized total return in USD over 20 years, while HDB and IBN have been among the strongest-performing emerging market bank ADRs. The ADR returns trail INR returns by 3–4 percentage points annually due to rupee depreciation, an important consideration for dollar-based investors.

## Why India outperformed China, Brazil, and Russia

The divergence from other major emerging markets is striking and structural, not cyclical.

**China's stock market paradox — high GDP growth, poor equity returns — stems from its market's design as a state financing tool rather than a shareholder wealth creation mechanism.** State-owned enterprises account for half the value of China's top 100 listed firms, prioritizing employment and policy goals over profitability. The Shanghai Composite peaked at 6,124 in October 2007 and still trades roughly 35% below that level nearly two decades later. The 2021 regulatory crackdown on technology, education, and real estate sectors destroyed an estimated $6–7 trillion in market value. The property crisis (Evergrande's $300+ billion default) devastated household wealth, while VIE structures expose 56% of US-listed Chinese companies to legal ambiguity. Household consumption drives just 40% of China's GDP versus 70% in India, and geopolitical tensions with the West create a permanent risk discount.

**Brazil's underperformance reflects commodity dependence, political instability, and currency destruction.** The Real has experienced repeated sharp depreciations, and chronic inflation forces the central bank to maintain among the world's highest real interest rates, making bonds structurally more attractive than equities. Political crises — Rousseff's impeachment, the Lava Jato corruption scandal, loss of investment-grade status in 2015 — repeatedly shattered investor confidence. MSCI Brazil declined 30% in 2024 alone amid fiscal panic.

**Russia's equity market effectively ceased to exist for foreign investors in 2022.** MSCI valued all Russian stocks at zero after the Ukraine invasion triggered comprehensive sanctions. The RTS Index had already demonstrated the risks of authoritarian governance: its 80% crash in 2008–2009, the 30% December 2014 collapse during Crimea sanctions, and the 39% single-day crash on February 24, 2022 — the fifth-worst single-day equity crash in history.

India's structural advantages form a clear contrast: democratic governance with an independent judiciary and central bank; a private-sector-led corporate landscape (only 18% SOEs in MSCI India versus 34% in MSCI EM overall); services-driven rather than commodity-dependent growth; English-speaking technical workforce supporting global IT operations; and digital public infrastructure that no peer matches. India's corporate earnings over five years surpassed those of the US by 76%, and consensus forecasts project India's three-year forward EPS CAGR at roughly **30% higher than the US**.

## Current valuations, risks, and the road ahead

As of early 2026, Indian markets are digesting a meaningful correction. The Sensex trades around **74,500–75,000**, roughly 13% below its September 2024 peak of 85,978. The Nifty 50's trailing P/E has compressed to **~20x** from above 24x at the peak, approaching its long-term consolidated average of 23x. The forward P/E of approximately **20.6x** remains at roughly one standard deviation above the five-year average but is more reasonable than the 24x peak. India's market capitalization stands at approximately **$5.1 trillion**, making it the world's fourth-largest equity market.

The near-term picture carries meaningful risks. **Record FII outflows** of ₹1.52 lakh crore in 2025 reflected rotation toward a rebounding China and higher US yields. **Nifty earnings growth decelerated sharply to 5–6% in CY2025** from the 20%+ pace of 2020–2024, triggering the correction. **Crude oil above $100/barrel** (with Brent briefly spiking to $119 amid Middle East tensions) strains India's current account given 85% import dependence. **US tariffs of 50%** on Indian goods introduce trade headwinds. The rupee hit a record low of ₹92.45 per dollar in early 2026.

However, the structural growth story remains intact. India's GDP is projected to grow **6.5–7.5% through 2030**, with S&P Global upgrading India to BBB from BBB- in its first sovereign upgrade in 18 years. Consensus Nifty targets for December 2026 cluster around **28,000–30,000**, implying 15–20% upside from current levels, contingent on earnings growth rebounding to the expected 17% in FY27. Monthly SIP flows of ₹25,000–31,000 crore provide a structural demand floor that previous market cycles lacked. India is projected to become the **world's third-largest economy by 2030 with a $7.3 trillion GDP**.

## Conclusion

India's near-parity with S&P 500 returns since 1997 is not accidental — it reflects the only large emerging market that simultaneously delivered strong GDP growth, favorable demographics, private-sector-led corporate earnings compounding, continuous reform, and institutional market development. The key insight for dollar-denominated investors is that **rupee depreciation has historically erased 3–4 percentage points of India's local-currency outperformance**, making the dollar-adjusted return roughly comparable to the S&P 500 rather than significantly superior. What distinguishes India is that it delivered S&P-equivalent returns while most other developed and emerging markets fell far short. The domestic investor revolution — ₹31,000 crore in monthly SIP flows, 207 million demat accounts, and DII ownership surpassing FII ownership — represents a structural regime change that makes India's market more self-sustaining than at any prior point. The current correction, driven by earnings deceleration and record FII outflows, has brought valuations closer to fair value, potentially creating an entry point for investors who believe India's demographic and digital dividends will continue compounding for the next decade.
