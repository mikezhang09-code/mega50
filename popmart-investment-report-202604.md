# Pop Mart: A $5 billion toy empire at an inflection point

**Pop Mart International (9992.HK) presents one of the most polarizing value debates in Hong Kong markets today.** After delivering revenue of RMB 37.1 billion (+185%) and net profit of RMB 12.8 billion (+308%) in FY2025, the stock paradoxically cratered 55% from its August 2025 all-time high of HK$339.80 to ~HK$152.70 — compressing the forward P/E to a record low of **10.3x** versus its three-year average of 24x. The sell-off reflects a market grappling with a single question: is Pop Mart a generational consumer brand or a fad at peak earnings? At current prices, the stock trades at a steep discount to analyst consensus targets of HK$250–260, yet the bear case — **38% revenue concentration in one IP** (Labubu), collapsing resale prices, and management guiding for just 20%+ growth in 2026 — remains credible. This report examines the full picture.

---

## From blind boxes to a global IP powerhouse

Pop Mart was founded in 2010 by **Wang Ning** in Beijing as a general lifestyle store. The pivotal transformation came in 2016 when Wang acquired the rights to Molly, a pouty-lipped character created by Hong Kong artist Kenny Wong, and packaged it in "blind box" format — sealed mystery boxes priced at RMB 59–69 (~$8–10) where buyers discover which figure they receive only upon opening. This gamification model drives repeat purchases, social media unboxing content, and collector communities. Revenue grew from RMB 158 million in 2017 to **RMB 37.1 billion in 2025** — a 235x increase in eight years.

The company now manages **100+ IPs** across its portfolio, with 17 generating over RMB 100 million in FY2025 revenue. Product categories have expanded beyond vinyl figures into plush toys (now the largest category at **50.4% of FY2025 revenue**), MEGA collector figures ($100–$960), accessories, jewelry, and lifestyle goods. Pop Mart operates **630 retail stores** and **2,637 Robo Shops** (automated vending machines) across 20+ countries, complemented by a robust online presence that topped the US App Store shopping chart in May 2025. The company holds **1,200+ trademarks, 1,600+ copyrights, and 45 patents**, with **97.6% of revenue from proprietary products** — a critical margin advantage.

Pop Mart's artist collaboration model is central to its moat. The company scouts designers globally through toy expos, social media, and open submissions. Artists retain creative control while Pop Mart handles production, marketing, distribution, and owns commercial rights, paying royalties. This ecosystem has attracted talent from Hong Kong, mainland China, Belgium, South Korea, and the US. The membership base reached **72.58 million registered members** by end of 2025, contributing 92% of total sales with a **55.7% repurchase rate**.

### Balance sheet and cash flow

Pop Mart's balance sheet is a fortress. The company carries **zero bank debt** with net cash of approximately **RMB 13.5–17 billion** as of FY2025 year-end. Inventory turnover improved to 83 days in H1 2025 from 133 days in FY2023, though full-year FY2025 showed inventory rising to RMB 5.4 billion with turnover days increasing to 123 — a potential warning sign of channel stuffing.

## The 55% decline: anatomy of a sentiment reversal

Pop Mart's stock rose from HK$10 (October 2022 trough) to HK$339.80 (August 26, 2025) — a **34x gain** — before collapsing to ~HK$152.70 by April 2026. The decline unfolded in three distinct phases.

**Phase 1 — Bernstein ignites doubt (October–December 2025).** On October 15, Bernstein initiated coverage with an Underperform rating, the sole bearish voice among 40+ analysts. Their thesis centered on declining secondary market volumes since June 2025, resale prices down 40%+ on select Labubu models, and comparisons to the Beanie Babies boom-and-bust. Google search interest for "Labubu" peaked in August 2025 and subsequently fell **~90%** by March 2026. The stock dropped 40% from its peak by December, with short interest rising to **6.3% of free float**.

**Phase 2 — Buyback-fueled rally (January–February 2026).** Pop Mart executed HK$347 million in share repurchases in January, sparking a 50% rebound to ~HK$273 by mid-February. This proved to be a dead-cat bounce.

**Phase 3 — Earnings crash (March 25–April 2026).** Despite delivering 185% revenue growth and 308% profit growth, the stock plunged **22.5% on March 25** and another **10.4% on March 26** — a cumulative 30.6% two-day wipeout. Three factors drove this:

- **Conservative guidance**: CEO Wang Ning guided for "no less than 20%" revenue growth in 2026, implying potential H2 deceleration on a high base — far below market expectations of 30–35%.
- **Labubu concentration worsening**: The Monsters' revenue share rose to 38% from 23%, the opposite of what investors wanted.
- **Inventory buildup**: Inventories surged to RMB 5.4 billion (from RMB 1.5 billion), with turnover days increasing 21% to 123 days.

Pop Mart responded with its **largest-ever single buyback** of HK$599.7 million on March 27, followed by three consecutive days of repurchases totaling HK$1.4 billion year-to-date through March.

## Growth catalysts that could reignite the stock

**International expansion remains the most compelling growth vector.** Overseas revenue grew from 9.8% of total in 2022 to 43.8% in 2025, and analysts project it could reach **69% by 2030**. The Americas grew 748% to RMB 6.81 billion in FY2025, yet Pop Mart has only 64 US stores versus a target of 100+ by end of 2026, with planned flagships on Times Square and Fifth Avenue. New manufacturing bases in Mexico, Indonesia, and Cambodia (operational January 2026) reduce tariff exposure and transit times.

**The Labubu movie with Sony Pictures** represents a potential IP lifecycle extension. Director Paul King's track record — Paddington and Wonka grossed $635 million+ globally — suggests strong commercial potential. If successful, this could transform Labubu from a collectible toy into a multi-decade entertainment franchise, following the Barbie and Pokémon playbook.

**IP diversification is real, if underappreciated.** While Labubu dominates, six IPs now exceed RMB 2 billion each, and 17 surpass RMB 100 million. The company's artist ecosystem, membership data (72.58 million members), and distribution infrastructure allow rapid scaling of new characters. The emerging Xing Xing Ren IP surged ~1,600% in 2025 to RMB 2.06 billion, demonstrating the platform's ability to incubate new hits.

**Adjacent category expansion** into theme parks (Pop Land in Beijing), jewelry, apparel, home goods, and even small home appliances (launching April 2026) broadens the revenue base beyond collectible toys.

## Five risks that keep the bear case alive

**Labubu fad risk tops the list.** Google search interest dropped 90% from its August 2025 peak. Resale prices on the secondary market collapsed — the "Big Into Energy" blind box fell from RMB 639 to RMB 100 (an 84% decline). Pop Mart deliberately scaled production to ~30 million units per month to combat scalpers, but this destroyed the scarcity premium that fueled speculative buying. The Beanie Babies comparison — a 1990s collectibles craze that imploded spectacularly — haunts the investment thesis.

**Revenue deceleration could be steeper than guided.** Management's 20%+ target for 2026 implies RMB 44.5 billion minimum, but Q4 2025 revenue of ~RMB 11 billion already showed a 9.5% sequential decline from Q3, with year-over-year growth slowing to ~120% from ~250% in Q3. If Labubu demand weakens further while no replacement IP scales proportionally, even the 20% target may prove optimistic.

**Regulatory headwinds in China** present ongoing uncertainty. China banned blind box sales to children under 8 in 2023 and required odds disclosure. A June 2025 People's Daily editorial called for stricter regulation, temporarily knocking the stock down 12%. While Pop Mart's core demographic is adults, tighter rules on pricing, marketing, or gambling-like mechanics could crimp growth.

**US tariff exposure** poses margin risk. Cumulative tariffs on Chinese goods reached 145% under the Trump administration. Pop Mart raised US prices on Labubu 3.0 from $22 to $28 and is diversifying manufacturing to Mexico and Southeast Asia, but the Americas (18.3% of revenue) remains exposed to trade war escalation.

**Corporate governance scores poorly.** ISS rates Pop Mart's governance quality at **10 out of 10 (worst possible)**, with particularly poor marks for board structure, shareholder rights, and compensation practices. Founder Wang Ning controls ~48.7% of shares, and the dividend payout ratio was cut from 35% to 25% despite record profits — a decision that frustrated income-oriented investors.

## Competitive moat: strong but not impregnable

Pop Mart's competitive position rests on several reinforcing advantages. The proprietary IP model generates **72.1% gross margins** — more than double Miniso's 32% — because Pop Mart owns the commercial rights to its characters rather than licensing from third parties. The nearest competitor, Miniso's TOP TOY brand, has effectively conceded the original IP race; TOP TOY founder Sun Yuanwen acknowledged that "the golden window for original IPs has already passed" and pivoted to licensed derivative works.

The distribution network of 630 stores, 2,637 Robo Shops, and integrated online channels across 20+ countries would take competitors years and billions to replicate. Pop Mart's membership ecosystem (72.58 million members contributing 92% of sales, 55.7% repurchase rate) creates meaningful switching costs. Manufacturing scale enables unit costs approximately **25% lower** than new entrants.

However, the moat is narrower than it appears. The blind box format is easily replicable — dozens of competitors from 52Toys to Sonny Angel to Funko's Mystery Minis employ the same mechanic. Customer crossover between Pop Mart and Miniso in the US is surprisingly low (high-single-digit percentage), suggesting the market may be more segmented than dominant. And crucially, Pop Mart's moat depends on continued IP relevance — a fundamentally unpredictable variable in consumer culture.

## Conclusion: compelling risk-reward with high uncertainty

Pop Mart at HK$152.70 is a fundamentally different proposition than Pop Mart at HK$339.80. The forward P/E of 10.3x prices in substantial deceleration, and the PEG of 0.27 suggests the market may be overcorrecting. A company generating **72% gross margins, 35% net margins, 44% ROIC, and sitting on RMB 13+ billion in net cash** with zero bank debt deserves attention from value investors.

The bull case requires believing that Pop Mart is building a durable consumer brand platform — a "Chinese Disney for collectibles" — rather than riding a single viral IP cycle. The evidence is mixed: six IPs exceeding RMB 2 billion and 43.8% international revenue suggest genuine platform value, but 38% concentration in Labubu with clearly fading viral momentum is concerning. The Sony movie deal, FIFA World Cup collaboration, and aggressive store expansion provide potential re-rating catalysts, but the **inventory buildup to RMB 5.4 billion** and conservative management guidance signal that even insiders expect normalization.

For value investors, the key question is whether the post-Labubu Pop Mart can sustain even modest growth (20-30%) while maintaining its extraordinary margin profile. If so, the current valuation is a bargain — analysts' median target of HK$250+ implies 65%+ upside. If Labubu follows the Beanie Babies trajectory and no replacement IP emerges at scale, the stock has further to fall despite seemingly cheap multiples. The answer likely lies somewhere between these extremes: Pop Mart has built genuine infrastructure and brand equity that transcends any single character, but the near-term earnings trajectory is more uncertain than at any point since the company's post-IPO trough in 2022.
